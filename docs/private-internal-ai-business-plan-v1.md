# Private Internal AI Brain Platform Business Plan v1

## Executive Summary

The broader business opportunity is not only CalDS as an oversight workflow. The larger platform is a private internal AI brain for document-heavy organizations that want AI over their own knowledge without exposing sensitive material to competitors, public model training, unmanaged employee tools, or uncontrolled vendor workflows.

The offer should be positioned as a governed brain layer for an organization, not a chatbot. Commodity copilots answer questions. This platform connects to the organization's documents and systems, respects permissions, cites sources, records decisions, supports review gates, and turns sensitive institutional knowledge into repeatable internal workflows.

CalDS remains the proof point because it already demonstrates the hard parts that serious buyers care about: provenance, bounded workflows, source-cited outputs, sentinel review, human approval, and a clear separation between truth, search, workflow, and agent reasoning. Those same design principles apply to enterprise knowledge, government oversight, sales enablement, compliance, pricing, procurement, operations, and executive decision support.

## Product Thesis

Every serious organization is accumulating more proprietary information than its people can reliably search, compare, and act on. The problem is not that they lack documents. The problem is that their documents, decisions, contracts, emails, SOPs, audits, claims, proposals, tickets, pricing histories, and compliance records are scattered across disconnected systems.

Generic AI tools are useful, but they are not enough for a company or government that needs private knowledge access, permission-aware retrieval, auditability, data boundaries, workflow control, and defensible outputs. Buyers will pay a premium when the system becomes infrastructure for how the organization thinks and operates.

The platform should therefore be sold as a private operating brain:

- It ingests and indexes internal knowledge.
- It respects the customer's access controls and confidentiality rules.
- It gives answers with citations and source paths.
- It routes work through governed workflows instead of free-form chat.
- It supports human review for sensitive decisions.
- It tracks usage, accuracy, provenance, and ROI.
- It can run in cloud, private cloud, or customer-controlled environments depending on the buyer's security posture.

## Product Lanes

### Private Enterprise Brain

This lane targets companies with large internal document stores and high-value proprietary knowledge. The first sale is not "AI transformation" in the abstract. The first sale is a measurable internal workflow where employees waste time looking for answers, rebuilding institutional memory, or asking senior people to interpret scattered material.

Strong first workflows include:

- Sales and proposal response from prior proposals, pricing history, product collateral, contracts, and customer notes.
- Customer support and field service from manuals, tickets, engineering notes, SOPs, and warranty history.
- Legal and compliance review from contracts, policy libraries, audits, incident records, and regulatory obligations.
- Operations and procurement from vendor files, purchase history, supply chain exceptions, plant procedures, and quality records.
- Executive knowledge from board materials, market updates, financial reports, strategy memos, and internal decisions.
- Engineering and product knowledge from specifications, change orders, issue logs, design files, and technical documentation.

The buyer gets a governed internal brain that helps people act on company knowledge without uploading sensitive materials into unmanaged AI tools.

### Government Oversight Brain

This lane builds directly from CalDS. The buyer is a government body, oversight committee, agency, audit function, inspector general-style office, or public-sector accountability team that needs evidence-bound review workflows.

Strong first workflows include:

- Grant and contract triage.
- Procurement anomaly review.
- Program performance review.
- Public-record evidence bundling.
- Legislative oversight preparation.
- Waste, fraud, and abuse screening with careful review language.
- AI governance and use-case inventory support.

The output is not an accusation engine. It is a reviewer-safe evidence workflow that compresses research time, preserves source context, and makes gaps explicit before humans decide what to do next.

### Codezan Deployment Lane

This lane treats Codezan as an implementation and relationship channel, not merely a development vendor. The current pipeline should be represented as anonymous warm access to tech and tech-adjacent buyers. Because no named targets are available yet, the plan should not invent company names, contacts, or committed opportunities.

The Codezan lane should focus on:

- Tech and tech-adjacent companies already discussing internal AI adoption.
- Service firms that own sensitive client documents and repeatable delivery playbooks.
- Software-adjacent businesses with support, implementation, sales, and engineering knowledge scattered across tools.
- Companies that want a private AI implementation but do not yet know how to scope security, retrieval, workflow, and governance.

Codezan can help convert warm interest into paid readiness sprints and pilots. The platform team provides the product thesis, governance architecture, pricing model, and proof point. Codezan provides technical implementation depth and network access.

### Recommended Pricing

The enterprise lane and government lane should not use one blended price table. Government can start with a similarly scoped proof of concept, but government-ready production should cost more because it carries procurement burden, security review, public accountability, auditability, records-retention concerns, and higher reputational risk.

### Enterprise Pricing

| Offer | Price | Buyer rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| Diagnostic / AI Readiness Sprint | $15k-$35k | Low-friction first step to inventory documents, rank use cases, define security posture, and select a pilot workflow. |
| Private Brain Pilot | $75k-$150k | Six- to eight-week implementation for one department or workflow with curated sources, citations, access rules, and measurable KPIs. |
| Production Deployment | $250k-$600k | Multi-connector implementation with SSO/RBAC, audit logs, evals, governance, admin controls, and production readiness. |
| Annual Platform Subscription | $120k-$600k/year | Recurring platform access based on users, document volume, connectors, security tier, and workflow count. |
| Managed Services | $10k-$40k/month | Ongoing optimization, connector maintenance, evals, prompt/workflow updates, reporting, and support. |
| Strategic Enterprise Account | $750k-$2M+ first-year TCV | Multi-department deployment with custom integrations, governance, dedicated support, and executive reporting. |

### Government Pricing

| Offer | Price | Buyer rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| Government Readiness / Procurement Sprint | $35k-$75k | Requires source policy, procurement language, security posture, stakeholder map, data-rights assumptions, and review workflow design. |
| Government Oversight Pilot | $150k-$300k | More expensive than a private enterprise pilot because public-sector work needs evidence policy, reviewer-safe language, audit trail, records posture, and staff-facing controls from day one. |
| Government-Ready Production Deployment | $500k-$1.25M | Covers security documentation, ATO/FedRAMP alignment where applicable, multi-role access, logging, retention posture, source provenance, training, and procurement artifacts. |
| Government Annual Platform / Support | $300k-$1.5M/year | Recurring platform, support, source updates, evaluation, reporting, user training, and governance operations. |
| Strategic Government Account | $1.5M-$5M+ first-year TCV | Multi-office, multi-agency, or statewide deployment with custom integrations, dedicated support, formal governance, and procurement complexity. |

This does not mean every public-sector conversation starts at $500k. A small legislative or committee proof of concept can be scoped lower if it uses public data, has no production ATO, and is explicitly labeled as an internal review pilot. But the moment the product is sold as government-ready infrastructure, it should carry a government premium.

### Why This Can Price Above Commodity Copilots

Commodity copilots are priced for broad access to a general AI assistant. This platform is priced for private knowledge deployment. The buyer is paying for the system around the model:

- Data mapping and ingestion.
- Permission-aware retrieval.
- Source citations and provenance.
- Workflow design.
- Review gates.
- Audit logs.
- Evaluation harnesses.
- Security posture.
- Custom connectors.
- Managed improvement.
- Implementation accountability.

The core commercial argument is simple: if the customer only wants generic chat, they should buy a commodity copilot. If they want their organization to think over its own sensitive knowledge safely, they need a governed internal brain.

## Go-To-Market Motion

### Entry Offer

The first offer should be the AI Readiness Sprint. It is short, concrete, and sales-friendly. It gives the buyer a way to move without committing to a full platform purchase.

The sprint should deliver:

- A document and system inventory.
- A risk and confidentiality map.
- A ranked use-case backlog.
- A first-workflow pilot design.
- A security and deployment recommendation.
- A pricing proposal for pilot and production.
- A buyer-ready ROI hypothesis.

### Pilot Offer

The pilot should be scoped to one department, one workflow, and one measurable result. The pilot must avoid becoming a vague AI sandbox.

Pilot acceptance criteria:

- At least one live document corpus or structured source.
- Source-cited answers.
- Permission-aware access design.
- Usage tracking.
- A small evaluation set.
- A named business owner.
- A measurable business outcome.
- A production conversion proposal.

### Expansion Offer

Expansion should happen by adding departments, connectors, workflows, and governance modules. Expansion should not require rebuilding the platform. The customer's first pilot should create the trust needed for a broader annual platform subscription.

## First Use-Case Prioritization

The best first workflow should satisfy four conditions:

1. It touches valuable proprietary knowledge.
2. It is painful enough that buyers already know the problem exists.
3. It can be measured inside 6-8 weeks.
4. It has a natural path to expansion.

Recommended first-use-case sequence:

| Priority | Use case | Why it sells |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sales/proposal brain | Clear ROI, fast cycle-time measurement, easy to explain to executives. |
| 2 | Compliance/legal brain | High pain, high risk, strong willingness to pay for control and traceability. |

| 4 | Government oversight brain | CalDS proof point, strong differentiation, but procurement cycles may be slower. |
| 5 | Executive decision brain | Valuable, but should usually follow a narrower workflow proof. |

### P&L Interpretation

The revised model is more conservative on margin because government work creates real delivery burden. That is the correct posture. Government-ready deployment should not be modeled like a normal enterprise SaaS account; it needs procurement support, security documentation, review workflow training, records posture, stakeholder alignment, and more senior delivery time.

The conservative case still requires tight burn control. The base case becomes profitable because at least one government production deployment and one government platform/support contract materially improve revenue. The upside case depends on a strategic account and repeatable deployment playbooks, not just more pilots.

The model favors a services-plus-platform motion in year one. Buyers with sensitive internal documents usually need architecture, data cleanup, connector work, security review, and workflow design before they will trust a recurring platform subscription. The managed-services line is not optional; it is how quality, security posture, prompt/workflow maintenance, evaluation, and customer success are funded.

## Red-Team Review

The plan is commercially plausible, but it has several weaknesses that should be fixed before it is used in a high-stakes sales or investor conversation.

| Risk | Why it matters | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| The word "brain" can sound like hype or imply full autonomy. | Serious buyers may hear "brain" as a vague AI metaphor or an unsafe autonomous system. | Use "private internal AI brain" in the headline, but define it operationally as a governed knowledge and workflow layer. Repeatedly state that it is not an autonomous decision-maker. |
| "Internal LLM" can be technically imprecise. | The product may use external foundation models, private endpoints, or customer-hosted models. Calling it an internal LLM could overpromise model ownership. | Use "internal AI knowledge layer" or "private AI brain over internal knowledge" unless the deployment actually includes customer-hosted models. |
| Pricing could be challenged as too high compared with $20-$30 copilots. | Buyers will compare against Microsoft, OpenAI, Claude, Amazon, and Google. | Explain that commodity seats are the model-access layer; this offer includes data mapping, connectors, permissions, evals, governance, review gates, and production support. |
| Pricing could also be too low for government-ready deployment. | Security, procurement, records, public accountability, and stakeholder review can consume more effort than the technical build. | Split enterprise and government pricing. Add a 25%-100% government premium once production, ATO/FedRAMP, records retention, or auditability are in scope. |
| Buyer map is broad. | A broad market can dilute the wedge and make the plan look unfocused. | Lead with two beachheads: Codezan tech/tech-adjacent warm pipeline for enterprise pilots, and CalDS-style public-sector oversight for government proof. Treat other industries as expansion segments. |
| Codezan pipeline is not named or quantified. | The P&L depends on access that is currently anecdotal. | Keep Codezan references anonymous and user-provided. Add a pipeline validation milestone: 10 discovery calls, 3 paid sprint proposals, 1 paid pilot. |
| Data quality is underpriced. | Messy documents, permissions, duplicates, scans, tables, and stale content can dominate costs. | Make the readiness sprint mandatory before fixed-price pilots unless the customer provides clean, scoped data. Add data remediation as a separate line item. |
| Security and compliance scope is underdeveloped. | Enterprise and government buyers will ask about SOC 2, FedRAMP, HIPAA, CUI, CJIS, FERPA, data residency, encryption, logging, and retention. | Add a security posture matrix during the sprint. Do not claim compliance certification until it exists. Sell alignment and documentation first. |
| P&L model assumes fast conversion. | Enterprise and government sales cycles can delay revenue by quarters. | Add a cash-conservative scenario with slower conversion, longer collections, and higher pre-sales cost. |
| Human review creates service cost. | The plan sells review gates but does not fully price reviewer enablement, training, and QA. | Include managed services and evaluation support in every production proposal. Make review workflow training part of deployment. |
| The plan could overbuild before finding the wedge. | A broad platform can consume engineering cycles before revenue proves the use case. | Sell one painful workflow first. Reuse the same architecture, but do not sell enterprise-wide transformation until one workflow works. |

### Red-Team Conclusion

The biggest correction is pricing discipline. Enterprise pricing is plausible at $75k-$150k for pilots and $250k-$600k for production when the buyer has scoped data and a clear workflow. Government-ready deployment should price higher because the true work includes procurement, governance, auditability, records posture, security documentation, public-risk management, and stakeholder training. The second correction is message discipline: sell the "brain" as a governed knowledge and workflow layer, not as a magical internal model.

## Why CalDS Matters As Proof

CalDS proves the product can handle sensitive, evidence-bound workflows where accuracy, traceability, and restraint matter. The existing architecture is directly relevant to enterprise private AI:

- Truth plane: keeps canonical records outside the model.
- Search plane: retrieves evidence without redefining truth.
- Workflow plane: handles state, approvals, retries, and pause points.
- Agent plane: coordinates bounded specialist reasoning.
- Sentinel review: prevents overclaiming and unsafe output.
- Human review: makes sensitive decisions explicit.

For enterprise buyers, these same controls translate into permission-aware answers, audit trails, trusted internal workflows, and safer adoption. For government buyers, they translate into reviewer-safe packets, source discipline, and accountable oversight workflows.
