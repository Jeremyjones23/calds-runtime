# Private Internal AI Brain Platform One-Pager v1

## The Simple Version

Every company and government has a hidden knowledge problem. Their most valuable information is spread across documents, contracts, emails, SOPs, policies, tickets, audit files, pricing notes, proposals, manuals, and internal systems. People waste time searching, guessing, asking senior staff, or recreating work that already exists somewhere.

Generic AI tools help with writing and brainstorming, but serious organizations need something more controlled. They need an internal AI brain that understands their own knowledge, follows their access rules, cites its sources, and does not turn sensitive internal material into unmanaged public-model input.

This platform is a governed brain layer for an organization, not a chatbot.

## What It Does

The platform connects to approved internal knowledge sources, indexes the material, respects permissions, and gives employees a private AI layer for asking questions, drafting work, comparing records, preparing decisions, and moving through governed workflows.

It is built around controls that serious buyers care about:

- Private deployment options.
- Source citations.
- Permission-aware retrieval.
- SSO and role-based access.
- Audit logs.
- Human review gates.
- Evaluation and quality checks.
- Workflow design around real business processes.

## Who Buys It

The best buyers are document-heavy organizations with proprietary knowledge they do not want to expose through unmanaged tools.

| Buyer type | Pain | First pilot |
|---|---|---|
| Tech and tech-adjacent companies | Internal knowledge is scattered across tools and teams | Company knowledge brain for support, sales, delivery, or engineering |
| Manufacturing, logistics, energy, construction | SOPs, maintenance records, vendor files, and quality docs are hard to search | Operations brain for one plant, region, or function |
| Legal, compliance, finance, insurance | Reviews are slow, repetitive, and sensitive | Contract, policy, audit, or claims brain with citations |
| Sales and service organizations | Teams recreate proposals, answers, and customer materials | Proposal and sales-response brain |
| Government and oversight bodies | Public records, grants, audits, and procurement files are hard to triage safely | Evidence-bound oversight workflow |
| Codezan warm pipeline | Tech and tech-adjacent prospects want internal AI but need a packaged offer | AI readiness sprint followed by one private brain pilot |

## Why It Is Worth More Than A Copilot Seat

Commodity AI seats clear around $20-$30 per user per month. Those tools are useful, but they are not the full implementation a serious organization needs.

This offer is premium because it includes the hard parts:

- Mapping sensitive data.
- Building connectors.
- Designing the workflow.
- Respecting permissions.
- Producing cited answers.
- Creating audit trails.
- Testing quality.
- Training users.
- Supporting production deployment.

If a buyer only wants generic chat, they should buy a commodity copilot. If they want a private AI brain over their own knowledge, they need implementation, governance, and a platform layer.

## Pricing

Enterprise and government should not be priced identically. Government pilots can start small, but government-ready production should cost more because procurement, auditability, public-record posture, security review, records retention, and stakeholder training add real work.

Public pricing anchors support the direction, but not every source is equal. The strongest anchors are vendor list prices and government procurement/labor sources: OpenAI Business at $20/user/month, Microsoft 365 Copilot at $30/user/month, Claude Enterprise self-serve at $20/seat/month plus usage, Amazon Q Business at $3-$20/user/month plus index capacity, Google Agent Search usage pricing, Progress Agentic RAG subscription tiers, and GSA labor/procurement vehicles. RAG implementation cost guides support five- and six-figure implementation pricing, but should be treated as market heuristics. LLM Labs is now supporting-only, not a primary pricing anchor.

## Revenue Model

The business should start with paid services and convert into recurring platform revenue.

1. Sell an AI Readiness Sprint.
2. Convert the sprint into a Private Brain Pilot.
3. Convert the pilot into Production Deployment.
4. Add Annual Platform Subscription.
5. Add Managed Services for optimization, support, evals, connectors, and workflow expansion.

The revised first-year base case is enterprise plus one government-ready conversion: 6 enterprise readiness sprints, 2 government readiness sprints, 5 enterprise pilots, 1 government pilot, 2 enterprise production deployments, 1 government production deployment, 3 platform/support contracts, and 3 managed-service customers. That supports roughly $4.44M in revenue and about $609k in operating contribution under the current planning model.

## Why CalDS Matters

CalDS is the proof point. It shows the system can handle sensitive work where source discipline, review boundaries, and auditability matter. The same architecture that makes CalDS safe for oversight work also makes the broader platform credible for enterprise internal AI:

- Truth layer for canonical records.
- Search layer for retrieval.
- Workflow layer for approvals and state.
- Agent layer for bounded reasoning.
- Sentinel review for sensitive outputs.
- Human review before high-risk conclusions.

That is the moat: governed AI over private knowledge, with evidence and controls built in from the start.

## Bottom Line

The opportunity is to help companies and governments build their own private internal AI brain. The wedge is one painful workflow. The expansion path is every department with sensitive documents, repeated decisions, and knowledge trapped in disconnected systems.

This is not a chatbot sale. It is the operating layer for how an organization uses its own knowledge safely.
